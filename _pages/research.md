<div style="text-align: justify;">
My research explores how bodily intention and movement can be sensed, externalized, and augmented through multimodal feedback and interactive systems. I am particularly interested in how physiological signals, haptic cues, and XR environments can be integrated to support embodied learning, somatic awareness, and skill training.

Situated at the intersection of embodied media, human–computer interaction, and physiological computing, my work investigates how subtle aspects of bodily action can be rendered perceptible through interactive technologies.
</div>

## Embodied Media and Somatic Interaction

A central line of my research investigates how embodied experiences—such as flow, force, and intentional extension—can be externalized through interactive systems. Traditional XR interfaces often focus on discrete gestures or visual feedback, while many embodied practices rely on subtle sensorimotor cues that are difficult to articulate or quantify.

Through projects such as *Qìfield*, I explore how palm-centered multimodal haptics can support the perception of directional force and bodily coordination in VR Tai Chi practice. By combining airflow, vibrotactile feedback, and EMG sensing, the system externalizes internal bodily states and enables users to experience movement dynamics that are otherwise difficult to perceive.

## Biosignal-Driven Adaptive Feedback

Another line of my research focuses on using physiological signals as dynamic control variables for adaptive interaction. Signals such as surface electromyography (EMG) can provide real-time information about muscular effort and motor intention, enabling interactive systems to respond to users’ internal states.

In the project *Towards EMG-Driven Pseudo-Haptic Feedback for VR Surgical Training*, EMG signals are used to estimate effort during simulated incision tasks. The system modulates visual, audio, and vibrotactile cues when the detected effort deviates from a target range, encouraging force awareness and self-correction during training.

## Hand-Centric XR and Multimodal Haptics

Hands play a central role in embodied interaction. My work investigates how hand-centered feedback can support spatial awareness, force perception, and movement coordination in XR environments.

I develop XR systems that integrate gesture recognition, multimodal haptic rendering (vibration, airflow, EMS), and embedded control pipelines. These systems allow hand movements, physiological signals, and actuation devices to be tightly coupled in real time, enabling new forms of embodied interaction and training.

## Research Methods and Systems

My research follows a research-through-prototyping approach. I design and build interactive systems that combine sensing, signal processing, and multimodal actuation to explore new forms of embodied interaction.

Typical system components include:

- XR prototyping platforms such as Unity and OpenXR-based systems
- Multimodal haptic rendering including vibration, airflow, and EMS
- Physiological sensing such as EMG and EEG
- Embedded systems based on ESP32 microcontrollers
- Real-time signal processing and inference pipelines

## Future Directions

Looking forward, I am interested in exploring how embodied interaction systems can support training, rehabilitation, and skill learning in XR. By integrating biosignal sensing, multimodal feedback, and adaptive interaction design, I aim to develop systems that make subtle bodily states perceptible and actionable.

Such systems may enable new approaches to somatic learning, expert skill training, and human–AI collaboration in embodied environments.
